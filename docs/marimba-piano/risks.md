# Risks

## Acoustic Risks

- The bar estimates may miss real pitch because stock density, grain, thickness, support holes, and undercut geometry dominate the final result.
  Mitigation: build one sacrificial coupon first and record correction factors before cutting a full octave.

- A shared resonator bank may not suit wood, synthetic, and aluminum cassettes equally.
  Mitigation: test aperture inserts first, then build a second pipe bank only if measurements show the first cannot be corrected.

- Bent resonators may lose energy or shift effective tuning.
  Mitigation: compare straight, bent, and downward-facing tubes with the same effective length before committing to packaging.

- Aluminum bars may sustain too long and blur fast playing.
  Mitigation: test smaller apertures, damping screens, and softer hammer faces before redesigning the chassis.

## Mechanical Risks

- A ganged hammer selector may add friction, noise, or unreliable half-index states.
  Mitigation: prove one hammer manually, then prove a small 3-note selector before building 13 notes.

- The platen lever may apply uneven pressure across the cassette.
  Mitigation: use adjustable stops, spring float in the resonator bed, and pressure/witness tests.

- Gaskets may take a compression set and lose the seal.
  Mitigation: use replaceable gasket strips and adjustable resonator standoffs.

- Cassette swaps may change strike alignment.
  Mitigation: use tapered pins, V-block rails, and visible strike targets during P1 setup.

## Fabrication Risks

- Batch-cutting bars from estimates could waste good stock.
  Mitigation: cut oversize, tune by measurement, and do not trust the table as final cut geometry.

- Full-keyboard scale may make selector torque and resonator packaging difficult.
  Mitigation: keep P1 to 13 notes and promote to 25+ notes only after mechanism data exists.

## Safety Risks

- Platen and selector mechanisms create pinch points.
  Mitigation: add hard stops, guards, and an interlock preventing cassette removal while clamped.

- Hard hammer faces can damage soft wood bars or launch fragments from brittle test pieces.
  Mitigation: use eye protection, sacrificial bars, and low-force tests before performance strikes.
